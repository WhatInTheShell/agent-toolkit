# Narrative Arc Templates

Three arcs cover the vast majority of professional presentations. Selection criteria are in SKILL.md. Use these as structural scaffolding — every section should be shrunk to the minimum number of slides that carries the argument.

---

## Arc 1: Problem → Solution → Proof

**Best for**: Technical audiences, peer reviews, skeptical stakeholders, grant applications, engineering postmortems.

**Logic**: Credibility in technical contexts is earned by demonstrating you understand the problem before you claim to have solved it. Leading with the solution before the problem reads as sales, not substance.

**Structure**:

| Position | Slide purpose | Title pattern |
|----------|--------------|--------------|
| 1 | Hook — the sharpest version of the pain | "X is broken in a way most teams haven't noticed yet" |
| 2–3 | Problem depth — evidence, scope, cost of inaction | "[Metric] is [direction] and the proximate cause is [specific thing]" |
| 4 | Decision pivot — what changes if we act vs. don't | "The window for low-cost intervention closes in [timeframe]" |
| 5–6 | Solution — mechanism, not just name | "We eliminate [root cause] by [specific change]" |
| 7–9 | Proof — data, benchmarks, prior work, failure modes considered | "Under [condition], the solution holds because [mechanism]" |
| Last | CTA — specific, time-bound, owner-named | "Decision needed: [action] by [date] from [role]" |

**Common failure mode**: Spending too many slides on problem and running out of time for proof. Cap problem at 2 slides maximum.

## Arc 2: Situation → Complication → Resolution (SCR)

**Best for**: Hostile or politically complex audiences, cross-functional presentations, situations where you don't control the audience's prior beliefs.

**Logic**: The SCR arc (McKinsey Pyramid Principle variant) creates common ground before introducing the complication. Both parties agree on the Situation; the Complication is why the current state is insufficient; the Resolution is the only logical response to that combination. Audiences that disagree with your conclusion but accept the Situation and Complication have to argue against their own logic to reject the Resolution.

**Structure**:

| Position | Slide purpose | Title pattern |
|----------|--------------|--------------|
| 1 | Situation — state of the world both parties accept | "Here is where we are today" (use data both sides already believe) |
| 2–3 | Complication — why the current state is insufficient | "This matters because [consequence] is arriving by [date]" |
| 4 | The decision forced by the complication | "Continuing as-is means [cost]; acting means [trade-off]" |
| 5–7 | Resolution — your recommendation with constraints acknowledged | "The path that addresses [complication] without [objection] is [X]" |
| 8+ | Evidence — supporting the resolution's feasibility | [Data, precedent, risk mitigation] |
| Last | CTA with explicit owner and timeline | |

**Non-obvious constraint**: The Situation slide must contain only facts the hostile audience already accepts as true. Introducing a contested fact into the Situation slide gives skeptics a foothold to derail the entire arc before you reach your argument.

## Arc 3: Vision → Gap → Path

**Best for**: Executive audiences, board presentations, fundraising, organizational strategy, product launches to business leadership.

**Logic**: Executives make decisions first and validate later. They don't have time to process data and synthesize conclusions — that's the presenter's job. Leading with vision gives them the conclusion to react to; the Gap shows why the status quo doesn't reach the vision; the Path is the investment ask. Data appears after the decision frame, as validation, not as input.

**Structure**:

| Position | Slide purpose | Title pattern |
|----------|--------------|--------------|
| 1 | Vision — the future state worth investing in | "[Outcome] is achievable in [timeframe] at [cost]" |
| 2 | Gap — the specific distance between now and the vision | "We are at [current state]; the gap is [specific delta]" |
| 3 | Cost of gap — why the gap is not acceptable | "[Gap] costs [specific number] per [period] and compounds" |
| 4 | Path — the sequence of decisions that close the gap | "Three decisions close the gap: [1], [2], [3]" |
| 5–7 | Supporting evidence — for any executive who wants validation | [Data for those who ask; executives who don't ask are already convinced] |
| Last | The single decision this audience needs to make | "Decision: [specific ask] by [date]" |

**Non-obvious constraint**: Never show more than 7 slides to an executive audience in a first-meeting context. If you have more evidence, put it in an appendix and offer it during Q&A. Showing more slides than the exec asked for signals that you don't know what's important.

## Hybrid Structures

**Technical content to a business audience (most common cross-functional case)**:

Use Vision → Gap → Path for the main deck (5–7 slides).  
Prepare a Problem → Solution → Proof appendix (5–10 slides) for the technical validation questions that always arrive in Q&A.  
Never mix the two arcs in the same slide sequence — it creates tonal whiplash.

**All-hands or town hall (large mixed audience)**:

Use Situation → Complication → Resolution.  
The Situation slide does double duty: it creates shared context for newcomers and signals "we're not pretending things are fine" to skeptics.  
Keep Resolution concrete and specific — vague aspirations in an all-hands are credibility-negative.
