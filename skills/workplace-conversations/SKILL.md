# Workplace Conversations

## Navigation — Choose Your Path

Before anything else, route to the right track:

```
What is the primary challenge right now?

A. I need to PREPARE and DELIVER feedback
   (structured message, written prep, SBI, async or scheduled 1:1)
   → Path A: Feedback Delivery

B. I am IN or about to enter a LIVE difficult conversation
   (real-time conflict, someone just escalated, amygdala hijack, Q&A under pressure)
   → Path B: Live Conversation Navigation

Not sure?
  → Is there a specific behavior + impact you want to communicate?
      YES → Path A
      NO, it's a relationship/conflict/emotional situation → Path B

Both paths lead to the same outcome: durable behavior change and an intact relationship.
```

## Path A: Feedback Delivery

### Mindset

1. **Feedback is information transfer, not judgment delivery.** The moment the receiver perceives evaluation, their prefrontal cortex partially shuts down — defensiveness is physiological, not character flaw. Frame as "I noticed / I want to understand" not "you did X wrong."

2. **Timing destroys or amplifies impact.** Feedback given within 48 hours of an event lands 3–4x more effectively than delayed feedback. "Annual review surprises" are managerial malpractice — they signal you withheld useful information for months.

3. **The goal of feedback is behavior change, not catharsis.** If you need to vent, do it elsewhere. The conversation exists to help them.

4. **Pattern vs. instance matters more than severity.** A single bad code review matters less than 3 in a row. Leading with "I've noticed a pattern" signals seriousness without catastrophizing a one-time event.

5. **Positive SBI is not trivial.** Most managers skip it or give generic praise. Specific positive SBI builds the safety account that makes corrective feedback land better later.

### Decision Tree for Feedback Type

```
Is this corrective or positive feedback?
  ├─ Corrective + first time → SBI + curiosity (explore root cause)
  ├─ Corrective + recurring pattern → SBI + explicit stakes + plan
  ├─ Corrective + HR-level severity → load references/delivery-scripts.md
  └─ Positive → SBI + specific impact (skip the "but" — never pair positive/corrective)
```

### Core Technique: SBI (Extended Practitioner Version)

The SBI formula: **Situation → Behavior → Impact**

**The "I" in Impact must connect to receiver values, not just your frustration.** If you know they care about team perception, connect the impact to that. Generic impact ("it slowed us down") lands weaker than specific impact ("it meant Sarah had to redo 3 hours of work and now doubts whether to surface issues").

**SBI+ for recurring patterns:**
> Situation → Behavior → Impact → **Expectation** → **Stakes**

Add: "Going forward, I need [specific behavior]. If this continues, [concrete consequence]." The stakes must be real and proportional — don't threaten what you won't follow through on.

For worked examples across code reviews, meetings, and deliverables: load `references/feedback-sbi-model.md`

### Timing Heuristics

| Scenario | Optimal Timing | Why |
|----------|---------------|-----|
| In-meeting behavior | Within same day, privately | Memory sharp; behavior still vivid |
| Code/work quality | Within 48 hours of delivery | Before they're deep in next task |
| Interpersonal conflict | After 2–4 hours cooling, same day | Enough distance to avoid flood; close enough to be concrete |
| Pattern (recurring issue) | Scheduled 1:1, not ad hoc | Signals importance, allows preparation |
| Crisis/incident behavior | After incident closes, not during | During crisis, feedback = distraction |

### Path A: When Things Go Wrong

| Situation | Likely Cause | Recovery |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Receiver goes silent or shuts down | Social threat response; they feel ambushed or shamed | Name it: "I notice you've gone quiet — I want to make sure this feels like a conversation, not a verdict. What's your reaction?" |
| Receiver becomes defensive / attacks back | Behavior stated as interpretation, not observation; or they feel blindsided | Return to facts: "I want to make sure I'm working from what I actually observed. Can we back up to [specific event]?" |
| Receiver agrees but nothing changes | Agreement was to end discomfort, not genuine buy-in | Explicit follow-through plan required: named actions, dates, check-in. "What specifically will you do differently by [date]?" |
| You realize mid-conversation you lack specifics | Feedback was prepared on impressions not evidence | Pause honestly: "I realize I should have more specific examples ready. Can we schedule 30 minutes tomorrow when I can come prepared?" |
| Positive feedback lands flat | Generic praise, or receiver distrusts motives | Increase specificity: name exact action, exact effect, exact why it mattered. Vague praise is discounted; surgical praise lands. |

## Path B: Live Conversation Navigation

### Mindset

1. **The goal is shared understanding, not a verdict.** Most failed conversations happen because one party enters to win. Practitioners enter to learn something they don't know yet — even when the facts seem clear.

2. **Timing is a power lever most people waste.** Raising an issue when the other person is stressed, public, or cornered guarantees defense. The right moment (private, unhurried, not post-incident) does half the work.

3. **A prepared BATNA changes how you show up.** Knowing what you'll do if the conversation fails — not as a threat, but as a real alternative — eliminates desperation from your posture. The other person feels it even if you never say it.

4. **Regression is normal.** Behavior changes after a hard conversation often reverse within 2–3 weeks. Schedule a check-in at week 3 precisely because of this, not because you distrust the other person.

5. **Specificity is protection.** Vague feedback ("you need to be more professional") creates defensiveness because it leaves the other person to guess. Specificity removes the guess and removes the threat.

### The Three Phases

**Phase 1: Preparation (40% of success)** — Most difficult conversations fail before they start.

Key preparation steps:
1. Separate facts from the story you're telling yourself
2. Identify your contribution — what did you do (or not do) that contributed?
3. Understand their perspective before you walk in
4. Define your goal: outcome goal, relationship goal, identity goal
5. Know your BATNA
6. Choose time and place (private, unhurried, not Friday)

For the full preparation worksheet: load `references/preparation-template.md`

**Phase 2: Delivery** — Opening sets the tone for everything.

Opening formula:
```
[Purpose] + [Intent] + [Invitation]
"I'd like to discuss [topic]. My goal is to [understand/resolve/improve]. I'd like to hear your perspective too."
```

Conversation structure: **Share → Listen → Explore → Resolve**

**Phase 3: Follow-up (Ensuring Change)**

- Send written summary within 24 hours — but ask THEM to summarize what they're taking away first. Their words reveal whether there's real alignment.
- Schedule a 3-week check-in at the close of the original conversation. Most regressions happen at week 2–3.
- Close every conversation with a specific, owned action: who does what, by when, and how you'll both know it happened.

### Amygdala Hijack Recovery

When you or the other person is triggered (raised voice, shutdown, flooding):

1. **Recognize it** — physical signs: heart rate up, breathing shallow, face flushing, thoughts racing
2. **Name it without blame:** "I notice this is hitting hard for both of us."
3. **Use the explicit pause:** "I want to make sure I respond thoughtfully. Can I have a moment?"
4. **Body reset:** Unclench jaw, drop shoulders, unfold arms, plant feet, lean back slightly
5. **Perspective shift:** What might be their positive intent? What am I missing?
6. **Exit if needed:** "I want to give this the attention it deserves. Can we take a break and continue tomorrow?"

Recovery time after full hijack: 20–30 minutes. Don't push through — a flooded conversation produces defensive agreements that don't stick.

### Path B: When Things Go Wrong

| Situation | Likely Cause | Recovery |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Other person shuts down or goes silent | Felt accused, not invited | Name it: "I notice you've gone quiet — I want to make sure this feels safe to discuss." Then pause. Don't fill the silence. |
| Conversation spirals into unrelated grievances | No shared agenda set upfront | Interrupt cleanly: "I want to hear all of this. Can we agree to finish this topic first and schedule time for the rest?" |
| Agreed actions don't happen after the conversation | Agreement was vague or socially coerced | Return with curiosity, not accusation. Ask what got in the way. Vague commitments usually mean the person didn't actually agree — they just ended the discomfort. |
| Person becomes hostile or escalates | Conversation revealed a threat to their status/livelihood | De-escalate: "I can see this is hitting hard. I'm not here to threaten your position — I'm here because I want this to work." Stop talking. Let them respond. |
| Your own emotions take over mid-conversation | Ambush response to something unexpected | Use the explicit pause. Practitioners normalize pausing; it signals self-awareness, not weakness. |

### Upward Feedback (Special Case)

Giving feedback to someone with power over you has a different risk profile. Three adjustments:

1. **Frame as impact on your effectiveness, not a critique of their behavior** — "When X happens, I find it harder to do Y" is harder to dismiss than "you do X."
2. **Request, don't demand** — end with a question, not a directive: "Would you be open to trying Z?" gives them an exit that isn't losing face.
3. **Accept that they may not change** — your goal is to have said it clearly once, not to fix them. If nothing changes, that's data for your own decisions.

### Conversation Types Quick Reference

| Type | Primary Risk | Key Move |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Performance issue | They feel blindsided | Cite prior informal conversations; ask what they'd need to succeed |
| Peer conflict | Both parties feel wronged | Separate fact from interpretation before the conversation, not during |
| Termination | Emotional flooding | Short, clear, no hedging; deliver the decision in the first 60 seconds |
| Salary/promotion | Negotiation feels adversarial | Anchor to market data + contribution, not personal need |
| Upward feedback | Power retaliation risk | See Upward Feedback section above |

## Shared NEVER Rules

- **NEVER use the feedback sandwich (positive-negative-positive)** — it trains people to distrust positive feedback and miss the corrective message. Use pure SBI instead.

- **NEVER give corrective feedback in public** — social threat activates the same neural circuits as physical threat. Public correction compounds shame and guarantees defensiveness.

- **NEVER delay feedback to "find the right moment" indefinitely** — memory encoding degrades within 72 hours. Waiting for perfect conditions means the specific behavior is no longer vivid to either party.

- **NEVER pair positive and corrective feedback in the same sentence with "but"** — "but" neurologically erases everything before it. Use separate conversations or at minimum separate paragraphs with a full stop.

- **NEVER give feedback when you or the receiver are emotionally flooded** — cortisol and adrenaline impair complex cognition. Reschedule explicitly: "I want to have this conversation when we're both at our best — can we do 10am tomorrow?"

- **NEVER interpret behavior aloud without evidence** — attributing motive ("you clearly don't care about quality") activates the fundamental attribution error. Stick to observable actions and their effects.

- **NEVER skip the "impact" step of SBI** — behavior without impact sounds like nitpicking. Impact connects the behavior to something the receiver actually cares about.

- **NEVER open with impact before the other person understands what behavior you're describing** — they'll spend the entire conversation defending their intention instead of hearing the impact. Behavior first, impact second, always.

- **NEVER have a high-stakes conversation without a written BATNA** — without one your desperation leaks into your tone and the other person calibrates to it.

- **NEVER follow up a difficult conversation only once** — single follow-ups let people wait you out. Set a 3-week check-in at the close of the original conversation.

- **NEVER raise a performance issue for the first time in a formal review** — the employee experiences it as an ambush, the feedback becomes legally and relationally contaminated, and you've lost 6–12 months of intervention time.

- **NEVER use "we need to talk" as a meeting invite subject** — it triggers 48–72 hours of anxiety that arrives to your conversation as pre-loaded defensiveness. Name the topic neutrally: "checking in on Q3 deliverables."

- **NEVER conflate "they heard me" with "they agreed"** — acknowledgment is not commitment. Close every conversation with a specific, owned action.

## Philosophy

Feedback is an act of respect — it assumes the person can change and is worth investing in. Withholding honest feedback to avoid discomfort is not kindness; it's abandonment.

The hardest conversations are hard because both parties have a legitimate version of events. Your job is not to correct their version but to build a third version together — one specific enough to produce durable action.

Deliver what's true, specifically, without cruelty.
